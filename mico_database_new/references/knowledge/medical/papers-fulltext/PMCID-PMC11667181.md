## Abstract

SummaryBackgroundMetabolic dysfunction-associated fatty liver disease (MAFLD) is associated with impaired regenerative capacity and poor postoperative prognosis following hepatectomy. Previous research has highlighted the importance of the gut–liver axis in the physiological and pathological processes of the liver. However, the contribution of gut bacteria to the regeneration of livers with MAFLD and its metabolic regulatory mechanisms remain elusive.MethodsPartial hepatectomy (PHx) was performed on C57Bl/6J mice fed with high-fat diet (HFD) for 12 weeks. Pathological examination, immunohistochemistry, and qRT-PCR analysis were performed to assess the severity of steatosis and proliferative potential. The gut microbiome was examined by 16S rRNA gene sequencing and shotgun metagenomics, whereas liver metabolomics was analysed via untargeted and targeted metabolomics using liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS).FindingsHFD-induced hepatic steatosis in mice led to impaired liver regeneration following PHx. The gut microbiota and liver metabolites were altered along with the liver regeneration process. Longitudinal time-series analysis revealed dynamic alterations in these data, whereas correlation analysis screened out bacterial candidates that potentially influence liver regeneration in MAFLD by modulating metabolic pathways. Among these bacteria, the dominant bacterium Akkermansia was selected for subsequent investigation. MAFLD mice gavaged with Akkermansia muciniphila (A. muciniphila) exhibited reduced liver lipid accumulation and accelerated liver regeneration, possibly through the regulation of the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle.InterpretationThese data demonstrated the interplay between the gut microbiome, liver metabolomics, and liver regeneration in mice with MAFLD. ## Full Text

### Evidence before this study

Epidemiological investigations have revealed the increasing global prevalence of metabolic dysfunction-associated fatty liver disease (MAFLD), and hepatic steatosis is known to be associated with an impaired regenerative capacity and poor postoperative prognosis after partial hepatectomy (PHx). Currently, there is substantial evidence for the correlation between MAFLD progression and the gut microbial community. However, limited data are available on the dynamic changes in the gut microbiota during the course of liver regeneration in MAFLD, as well as its metabolic regulatory mechanisms.

### Added value of this study

Significant alterations in gut microbiome and liver metabolomics were observed in high-fat diet induced MAFLD mice undergoing PHx. Time-series analysis provides an intuitive approach for demonstrating the dynamic fluctuations of datasets over time. The microbiota and metabolites were divided into separate clusters according to the different abundance trends over time, and those that were consistent with the liver regeneration rate were selected for further investigation. Spearman correlation analysis was used to screen out bacterial candidates that affected MAFLD liver regeneration through metabolic pathways. Notably, Akkermansia appeared as the top species in the screened microbial cluster and was positively correlated with TCA cycle metabolites. We then confirmed the ability of Akkermansia muciniphila to suppress hepatic inflammation and lipid accumulation and promote post-PHx liver regeneration in MAFLD mice.

### Implications of all the available evidence

This study combined microbiome and metabolomic profiling to screen specific bacterial targets that may affect liver regeneration in MAFLD. We provide a theoretical basis for promoting postoperative MAFLD liver regeneration by modulating gut microbiota with probiotic supplementation.

### Introduction

Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) encompasses a spectrum of pathological conditions, from simple steatosis to steatohepatitis without the influence of secondary causes, and can develop into non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NASH) and cirrhosis.1,2 In most epidemiological studies, the global prevalence of NAFLD is estimated to be 25% and continues to increase in parallel with the worldwide obesity epidemic.3 NAFLD is known to have a close correlation with obesity and often coexists with metabolic comorbidities.3,4 In a recent consensus publication by Eslam et al., it was proposed that NAFLD should be reclassified as metabolic dysfunction-associated fatty liver disease (MAFLD), which is considered a more suitable and inclusive terminology.5

Partial hepatectomy (PHx) is an effective treatment for a range of liver diseases, including benign and malignant tumours, intrahepatic duct calculi, and abscesses. Hepatic steatosis is known as a risk factor for poor prognosis after PHx, and fat accumulation disrupts hepatocyte homeostasis, which may result in increased postoperative mortality and complications.6,7 Clinically, approximately over 20% of patients requiring liver resection present with varying severity of steatosis.6 Notably, individuals with moderate to severe hepatic steatosis demonstrate significantly impaired liver regenerative capacity after PHx, and clinicians should exercise caution when evaluating the extent of liver resection.7, 8, 9, 10 In murine experiments, numerous studies have also documented the inhibitory effects of steatosis on liver regeneration in both genetic and diet-induced MAFLD models.11, 12, 13 Therefore, identifying intervention targets to promote liver regeneration after PHx in patients with MAFLD is meaningful.

The gut–liver axis denotes bidirectional crosstalk between the gut and liver, encompassing the reciprocal regulation of metabolites, microbial antigens, nutrients, and bile acids.14 The pivotal role of the gut–liver axis in the pathogenesis of hepatic steatosis is well established.15, 16, 17 Multiple mechanisms link microbial dysbiosis and MAFLD, including choline deficiency, increased intestinal permeability, and alterations in short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) synthesis and bile acid composition.15 In addition, the complex relationship between gut microbiota and liver regeneration has attracted much attention. As early as 1985, Cornell et al. discovered that gut sterilization impaired liver regeneration after 67% liver resection in rats.18 Liver regeneration is accompanied by significant metabolic remodeling,19 and rapid advances in metabolomic techniques in recent years have offered a novel perspective on the mechanisms of gut microbes in diseases and physiological processes. Liu and colleagues have demonstrated relevant associations between hepatic gene expression profiles and liver metabolism with changing gut microbiota during liver regeneration, where gut-derived metabolites, such as SCFAs and bile acids, play important roles.20 However, there is currently a lack of studies investigating the impact of gut microbiota on postoperative liver regeneration in MAFLD as well as the potential metabolic mechanisms involved.

In this study, we employed a combination of gut microbiome and liver metabolomics to elucidate the role of the gut microbiota in postoperative liver regeneration in MAFLD and preliminarily explored the underlying metabolic mechanisms. We also identified and validated a potential probiotic, Akkermansia muciniphila (A. muciniphila), which may be beneficial for facilitating postoperative recovery in obese individuals with MAFLD (Fig. S1).

Liver samples were immersed in 4% paraformaldehyde solution for 48 h and embedded in paraffin wax before sectioning. The percent area of Oil Red O and the percentage of Ki67-positive cells were quantified using ImageJ software (NIH, https://imagej.net/software/fiji/).

### Immunoblotting

Liver tissue fragments were lysed with RIPA solution (P0013C, Beyotime, China) containing protease and phosphatase inhibitor cocktails, followed by ultrasonication on ice, and centrifugated for 15 min (14,000 g, 4 °C). The total protein concentration was measured using a BCA Protein Assay Kit (23225, Thermo Scientific, USA). 10 μL lysate per lane (corresponding to 30 μg protein) was separated by SDS-PAGE gels (4–20%) and transferred to the polyvinylidene difluoride membrane. Membranes were blocked (5% fat-free milk in Tris-buffered saline with 0.5% Tween 20) and incubated with primary antibodies to PCNA (1:5000; ab92552, RRID: AB_10561973, Abcam) and β-tubulin (1:5000; A12289, RRID:AB_2861647, ABClonal) overnight at 4 °C, followed by goat anti-rabbit secondary antibody for 1 h (1:5000; ab97051, RRID:AB_10679369, Abcam) at room temperature. Signals were visualised using enhanced chemiluminescence and an ECL Western blot kit (FD8020, Fdbio science, China) and quantified on ImageJ software. All antibodies had been validated by Western blot analysis.

### Quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR)

Total RNA was extracted from liver tissue using FastPure® Cell/Tissue Total RNA Isolation Kit (RC112-01, Vazyme, China), followed by cDNA synthesis using the HiScript III All-in-on RT SuperMix Perfect for qPCR (R333-01, Vazyme). Relative quantification was performed using the ChamQ Universal SYBR qPCR Master Mix (Q711-02, Vazyme). The primer sequences are listed in Table S1. Expression was quantitated by the 2−ΔΔCt method.

### Serum biochemical measurements

Serum albumin (ALB), triglyceride (TG), total cholesterol (TC), aspartate aminotransferase (AST), alanine aminotransferase (ALT), alkaline phosphatase (ALP); total bilirubin (TBIL), direct bilirubin (DBIL), lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), and total bile acid (TBA) levels were measured using commercial kits (Ningbo Saike Biological Technology Co., Ltd., China). Lipopolysaccharide (LPS) level was assessed with enzyme linked immunosorbent assay (CSB-E13066m; CUSABIO, China).

### 16S rRNA gene amplicon sequencing of faecal microbiota

Stool samples were snap frozen in liquid nitrogen and kept at −80 °C until needed. Total genomic DNA was extracted from faecal samples using the CTAB/SDS method, and its concentration and purity were confirmed by agarose gel electrophoresis. PCR amplification of the V3–V4 region of the 16S rRNA gene was conducted using specific primers (F: CCTAYGGGRBGCASCAG; R: GGACTACNNGGGTATCTAAT), followed by amplification, purification, and qualification steps. The NEBNext® Ultra™ IIDNA Library Prep Kit was utilised for library construction, followed by sequencing on the NovaSeq6000 platform. Sequencing reads were trimmed and assembled using FLASH software (Version 1.2.11) and processed with fastp (Version 0.20.0), and chimeras were removed using USEARCH software (Version 10).21,22 Finally, QIIME2 software (Version QIIME2-202006) was utilized for downstream analysis.

### Metagenomic shotgun sequencing

The samples were fragmented into segments of 350 bp and further processed with end repair, polyA-tailing, ligation of sequencing adapters, PCR amplification, and purification to construct the library. The size of the library fragments was assessed using Fragment Analyzer (AATI), quantified (effective library concentration >3 nM) using Q-PCR, and subjected to PE150 sequencing. Clean data were obtained using Fastp (https://github.com/OpenGene/fastp) and assembled using the MEGAHIT software. DIAMOND software (https://github.com/bbuchfink/diamond/) was used for species and function annotation using the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) database (http://www.kegg.jp/kegg/).

### Metabolomics analysis

Liver tissues were pulverised in liquid nitrogen and dissolved in 80% methanol. After incubating on ice for 5 min, samples were centrifuged (15,000 g, 4 °C, 20 min) and diluted to 53% methanol concentration, and centrifuged again (15,000 g, 4 °C, 20 min). The supernatant was collected and injected into the liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) system for analysis.

The UHPLC-MS/MS analysis was conducted using the Vanquish UHPLC system (Thermo Scientific) and Orbitrap Q ExactiveTM HF mass spectrometer (Thermo Scientific). Samples were injected into a Hypesil Gold column (100 × 2.1 mm, 1.9 μm) using a 12-min linear gradient at a temperature of 40 °C (0.2 mL/min). For positive polarity mode, Eluent A (0.1% FA in water) and eluent B (methanol) were used, and for negative polarity mode, eluent A (5 mM ammonium acetate, pH 9.0) and eluent B (methanol)were used. The solvent gradient was performed with 2% B, 1.5 min; 2–85% B, 3 min; 85–100% B, 10 min; 100–2% B, 10.1 min; 2% B, 12 min. Then, the Q ExactiveTM HF mass spectrometer was operated with a sheath gas flow rate of 35 psi, spray voltage of 3.5 kV, and capillary temperature of 320 °C. Raw data were imported into Compound Discoverer 3.1 (CD3.1, Thermo Scientific) for peak detection and alignment. Statistical analyses were performed using Python (version 2.7.6) and R (version R-3.4.3).

The metabolites were annotated using the KEGG (https://www.genome.jp/kegg/pathway.html) and LIPID MAPS (http://www.lipidmaps.org/) database.

### Statistical analysis

Statistical analyses were performed using Student's t-test and Mann–Whitney U test. Differences with P < 0.05 were considered statistically significant (ns = statistically not significant, P > 0.05, ∗P < 0.05, ∗∗P < 0.01, ∗∗∗P < 0.001, ∗∗∗∗P < 0.0001). The TimeSeriesExperiment package and Mfuzz package in R were used for cluster analysis. The correlations were tested using Spearman's correlation coefficients. Data are expressed as the mean ± SEM using GraphPad Prism 9.0 (GraphPad Software, USA).

It is of notice that the abundance of Akkermansia, the dominant bacterium of Cluster 3, showed a significant positive correlation with citric acid, α-KG, D-2HG, dl-glutamine, and N-[4-(diethylamino) phenyl]-N′-phenylurea (P < 0.05) (Fig. 6A). The majority of these metabolites were closely associated with the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle. Both citric acid and α-KG are key intermediates in the TCA cycle, while glutamine is known to enter the TCA cycle via ɑ-KG and serves as a source of NADPH.27 D-2HG is also a mitochondrial metabolite derived from α-ketoglutarate.28 The TCA cycle is indispensable for aerobic glycolysis and provides ATP, lipids, and nonessential amino acids for rapid cellular proliferation.29 Deranged ATP homeostasis is a critical factor in the impaired regeneration of fatty livers.6 Taken together, these six microbes were screened out to be potentially associated with MAFLD liver regeneration, and Akkermansia could be an attractive candidate that may function by affecting the TCA cycle.

### A. muciniphila rescues liver regeneration in HFD mice

Metagenome sequencing provides higher taxonomic resolution and offers functional insights into the microbial community.30 To identify liver regeneration-related bacterial species in MAFLD mice, we performed shotgun metagenomic sequencing of faecal samples from HFD mice before and on the third day after PHx, and found the upregulation of A. muciniphila, the most prominent species in the genus Akkermansia, at the species level (Fig. 6B). The important role of A. muciniphila in gut barrier protection and metabolic regulation is well recognised, and it is known as a next-generation probiotic (NGP). Studies have indicated a declined abundance of A. muciniphila in both mice and patients with MAFLD.31 Interestingly, our data showed an abnormal dominance of Akkermansia during the postoperative liver regeneration process, and the correlation analysis indicated a close association between Akkermansia and the TCA cycle. MetagenomeSeq analysis based on the KEGG database also revealed that the relative abundance of certain metabolic pathways, including carbohydrate metabolism, was higher on Day 3 compared to Day 0 (Fig. 6C). Consequently, A. muciniphila was considered for further validation to explore its potential in promoting postoperative liver regeneration in HFD-induced MAFLD mice via oral probiotic administration.

We validated the therapeutic effects of A. muciniphila in HFD and CD-HFD mouse models and observed similar effect in germ-free HFD mice. ### Discussion

Despite the acknowledged significance of the gut microbiota in influencing MAFLD progression,32 limited information is available on the role of bacteria in post-hepatectomy regeneration in MAFLD. This study recorded the liver regeneration process and changes in liver function following PHx in mice with HFD-induced MAFLD. We demonstrated alterations in gut microbiota and liver metabolites over the course of post-PHx regeneration in mice with MAFLD. An analysis combining gut microbiota and liver metabolites indicated the potential underlying mechanisms by which the gut microbiota influence liver regeneration in obese mice. Furthermore, MAFLD mice treated with oral A. muciniphila exhibited substantial amelioration of lipid accumulation, inflammation, and liver regeneration. Our study provides new insights for facilitating postoperative recovery in patients with MAFLD undergoing liver resection.

Accumulating evidence has demonstrated that the gut microbiota can influence various physiological and pathophysiological processes in the liver, including steatosis and liver regeneration.15,20 Our study explored the potential involvement of the gut microbiota in the liver regeneration process in HFD-induced MAFLD. Significant changes in the gut microbiota were observed after PHx, with an overall recovery trend over time. Further analyses focused on the composition of gut microbiota in HFD-induced MAFLD mice. Dubosiella was found to be one of the most prominent bacteria (20.29%) in the faeces of MAFLD mice, consistent with previously published findings that Dubosiella was strongly correlated with NASH progression.33 After PHx, Escherichia-Shigella quickly became dominant (comprising 68.19% on the first day). Previous publications have shown a strong correlation between Shigella and inflammation,34 which supports our observations in the present work. Moreover, we noticed a temporary increase in the relative abundances of Muribaculaceae, Bacteroides, and Akkermansia. A community of strains including Muricbaculum has been reported to regulate lipid metabolism and stimulate liver regeneration,35,36 which may explain its persistent increase at the late stage of the liver regeneration process. Both Bacteroides and Akkermansia are closely associated with SCFAs production.31,37 In addition to their involvement in providing energy sources and regulating the host immune system, SCFAs generated by gut bacteria also play critical roles in lipid metabolism and liver regeneration through the gut–liver axis.35 Bacteroides is the main producer of propionate (one of the three major SCFAs in the human gut), and Akkermansia promotes the secretion of acetic, propionic, and butyric acids.31 Moreover, Akkermansia started to increase at the onset of liver regeneration in MAFLD mice and became the predominant bacterium (30.60%) when the regeneration rate peaked (on the third day after PHx). Akkermansia was further shown to be positively associated with liver regeneration rate in MAFLD mice in the time-series analysis of this study. As a promising probiotic with therapeutic efficacy against inflammation, metabolic disorders, and liver diseases, the depletion of Akkermansia has previously been demonstrated in animals and humans with MAFLD.38 Therefore, the abnormally high levels of Akkermansia during liver regeneration in MAFLD mice in our study deserve further attention. Collectively, our study detected changes in gut microbiota during liver regeneration in MAFLD and may provide new bacterial targets for postoperative recovery.

There is an intricate metabolic interplay between the gut and liver.14,39 Numerous studies have been conducted to elucidate the mechanisms by which the gut microbiota regulate liver function and pathology.40 A recent study showed that SCFAs provided by gut bacteria were functionally important for the growth and proliferation of hepatocytes.35 To gain preliminary insights into the underlying metabolic mechanisms involved in the positive effect of microbiota on liver regeneration in MAFLD, we performed liver metabolomics and correlation analyses. The analysis revealed a close association between bacterial species abundance in Cluster 3 of the microbiome and metabolite abundance in Cluster 6. Six bacteria exhibited a positive relationship with the metabolomics. Blautia was positively related to GMP, while others were predominantly positively correlated with TCA cycle metabolites such as citric acid, α-KG, D-2HG, and glutamine. GMP, the ultimate product of purinosomes, provides material for DNA synthesis and cell proliferation. The TCA cycle functions in the mitochondria, providing ATP and precursors for lipid, amino acid, and nucleotide synthesis for rapid cell proliferation.27 ATP plays a critical role in G1/S cell cycle transition during liver regeneration; however, a reduced ability to synthesise ATP due to mitochondrial dysfunction occurs in fatty hepatocytes.41,42 Our study identified the bacteria associated with MAFLD liver regeneration and their potential metabolic relevance.

Gut microbiota-based therapies such as probiotic therapy are believed to alleviate liver disease and promote liver regeneration.40 A. muciniphila, a strictly anaerobic bacterium abundant in the human intestinal microbiota, has been implicated in beneficial roles in MAFLD by restoring the gut barrier and gut–liver axis.31 However, there are no reports on the function and mechanism of action of A. muciniphila in liver regeneration. Therefore, we aimed to determine whether A. muciniphila played a direct causative role in enhancing cell proliferation after PHx in MAFLD. Compared with the control group, oral administration of A. muciniphila mitigated the development of MAFLD induced by HFD or CD-HFD, manifested by reduced rapidity of body/liver weight gain and decreased serum TG and ALT levels, which was consistent with observations in previous studies.43 Moreover, microbiota intervened with A. muciniphila accelerated hepatocyte proliferation in MAFLD mice. The phenomenon was similarly observed in germ-free HFD mouse model, excluding the influence of other confounding factors. These findings suggested that in addition to its preventive effects on MAFLD, A. muciniphila can partially mitigate delayed liver regeneration in mice with MAFLD. However, whether the ability of A. muciniphila to promote liver regeneration is related to the alleviation of hepatic steatosis remains to be further investigated.

Nevertheless, some limitations of our study should be recognized. First, there is a disparity in the gut microbiota between humans and mice species; therefore, collection and further investigation of human specimens are warranted. Additionally, although we selected clusters that were positively correlated with liver regeneration, it is plausible that microbes negatively correlated with liver regeneration rates (potentially exerting harmful effects) could also be meaningful. The removal of such microbes may also have a positive effect on liver regeneration.

In summary, the present study demonstrated alterations in gut microbiota and liver metabolism during liver regeneration in HFD-induced MAFLD mice. Gut microbiota may influence the liver regeneration progression through metabolic pathways. MAFLD and liver regeneration were ameliorated by oral administration of A. muciniphila. This study highlights that manipulation of the gut microbiota may serve as a therapeutic strategy to promote recovery after PHx for MAFLD.
