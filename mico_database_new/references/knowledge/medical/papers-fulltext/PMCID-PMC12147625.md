### Introduction

Colorectal cancer (CRC) is the second leading cause of cancer-related mortality worldwide, with a gradual increase in incidence among individuals under 50 years of age [1]. Recent studies have confirmed a close association between the gut microbiota and CRC development [2]. The gut microbiota has been extensively acknowledged to impact diverse physiological processes of the host, such as metabolism, neural transmission, and modulating the host immune microenvironment through the regulation of local and systemic immune responses [3].

The pivotal significance of gut microbiota in the pathogenesis of CRC has been progressively acknowledged over the past decade. Emerging evidence has shown that individuals with CRC exhibit dysregulated microbial structures, particularly in bacterial microbiota [4], [5]. For example, certain bacteria that are believed to promote carcinogenesis, such as Fusobacterium nucleatum, Escherichia coli, and Peptostreptococcus have been found to be more abundant in the feces of CRC patients [6], [7]. Conversely, beneficial genera, such as Akkermansia muciniphila (A. muciniphila), Faecalibacterium, and Bifidobacterium have been observed to be decline [8]. A. muciniphila is a symbiotic inhabitant of the mucosal layer of the intestinal tract and is considered, a promising probiotic candidate. A. muciniphila plays a significant role in enhancing host metabolic functions and immune responses [9]. In addition, as bacteria colonize and proliferate in the gut, they produce a series of metabolites that impact host metabolism and immune responses. Bile acids (BAs) are vital microbial metabolites that regulate homeostasis in the immune microenvironment of the gastrointestinal tract. Microbiota-mediated BA metabolism in the gut can influence the expression of chemotactic factors, thus modulating the accumulation of immune cells and the anti-tumor immunity of the liver [10]. Bacterial metabolism of BAs produced in the liver generates secondary BAs, that can regulate intestinal inflammation and the occurrence of intestinal tumors [11]. Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA), as an important secondary BA, can ameliorate disease progression issues in patients with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) and reduce the risk of adenoma recurrence [12]. Given the close association between the gut microbiota and CRC, manipulation of gut microbiota has emerged as a promising strategy for the treatment of CRC.

In recent years, the interaction between the gut microbiota and natural products has become a pivotal area of research in understanding the anti-tumor mechanisms of natural products [13]. Studies have already confirmed that dietary polyphenols can regulate appetite through the gut-brain axis and gut homeostasis [14]. Numerous studies have revealed a correlation between natural products and gut microbiota. Gut microbiota have the capability of metabolizing natural products, thus influencing their bioavailability and antitumor efficacy [15], [16], [17]. Conversely, natural products have the potential to prevent and cure diverse types of malignancies by modulating gut microbiota [18]. Panax ginseng, hailed as the “King of Herbs,” is a traditional medicinal herb with a long-standing reputation in herbal medicine. It contains various bioactive compounds, including ginseng polysaccharides, ginseng polyphenols, and ginsenosides, with ginsenosides being the primary active constituents in ginseng [19], [20]. Although the content of rare ginsenosides is less than 0.1 %, they possess higher bioavailability and stronger biological activity. Rare ginsenosides have made remarkable progress in the field of medicine [21]. Rare ginsenoside Rh4 (Rh4) has the ability to induce tumor cell apoptosis and can ameliorate antibiotic-induced gut dysbiosis and intestinal inflammation [22], [23], [24]. However, the impact of Rh4 on CRC and the associated gut microbiota remains unknown.

In this study, we focused on gut microbiota to explore the effects of Rh4 on CRC. We used the Azoxymethane/Dextran Sodium Sulfate (AOM/DSS) model of colitis-associated cancer and employed transcriptomics, genomics, and metabolomics techniques to investigate the inhibitory impact of Rh4 on CRC. Moreover, we systematically probed the role of Rh4-gut microbiota crosstalk in treating CRC using antibiotic treatment and fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) experiments. Finally, we elucidated the pivotal roles played by key bacterial species and metabolites regulated by Rh4 in CRC. Taken together, our findings suggest that Rh4 is a promising modulator of gut microbiota for CRC treatment.

### AOM/DSS experiment

Male C57BL/6J and BALB/C mice were procured from the Air Force Medical University. C57BL/6 mice were administered AOM/DSS while being fed a standard mouse chow. AOM was intraperitoneally injected at a dose of 10 mg/kg. One week after the AOM injection, the mice were administered 2.0 % DSS in their drinking water for a week, followed by normal drinking water for two weeks. This cycle was repeated three times as previously reported [25]. The Rh4 group (60 mg/kg Rh4, i.g.) received Rh4 once daily, as illustrated in Fig. 1A.

### Germ-free mouse experiment

For the germ-free mouse experiment, an antibiotic cocktail containing 0.2 g/L of ampicillin, neomycin, and metronidazole, as well as 0.1 g/L vancomycin, was added to the drinking water throughout the experiment as previously reported [26]. Water with antibiotics was renewed every two to three days to guarantee its potency.

### FMT administration

For the FMT experiment, the recipient mice were treated with an antibiotic cocktail for two weeks. The donor mice were treated with 60 mg/kg of ginsenoside Rh4 for three weeks. After that, fresh feces were collected from the donor mice every day and FMT was performed. Fecal samples (600 mg) were quickly and evenly mixed with 6 mL sterile saline, centrifuged at 1000 x g, and the supernatant was collected. Finally, 200 µL of the bacterial suspension was transferred to each recipient mouse via intragastric administration within a time frame of 10 min, as previously described [27].

### Administering a. Muciniphila orally

In the A. muciniphila colonization experiment, the mice were gavaged with A. muciniphila at a concentration of 1 × 109 CFUs every day for three weeks; after the end of the experiment, mouse feces were collected for bile acid metabolomic analysis.

### Oral gavage with UDCA administration

In the UDCA anti-tumor experiments, the first step was to establish a tumor-bearing syngeneic model where all BALB/C mice were inoculated with CT26 cells (2 × 106) on the right flank. After one week, mice with tumors measuring 50 mm3 were divided into two groups. Mice in the Model group received daily oral gavage of saline. The UDCA group (100 mg/kg UDCA, i.g.) received UDCA once daily.

### Intestinal permeability assay

The integrity of the intestinal barrier was assessed by conducting an in vivo permeability test using FD4. Following oral administration of FD4, mouse blood was collected after 4 h, centrifuged, and serum was collected in a new centrifuge tube. The concentration of FD4 in the serum was measured using a fluorescence spectrophotometer. The results of the sample measurement, along with the FD4 concentration standard curve, were input into data analysis software to determine the FD4 content of the samples.

### Histopathological staining

The excised colon tissues were fixed in formalin for a period of 48 h. Following fixation, the tissues were embedded in paraffin and sliced into sections with a thickness of 5 μm. The sections were dewaxed using a series of xylene solutions and rehydrated using graded ethanol solutions.

### H&E staining

The paraffin-embedded colon tissue sections were subjected to H&E staining and examined under a light microscope by observers who assessed the histological scores.

### Periodic acid-schiff stain (PAS)

AB-PAS staining was performed to detect goblet cells, following the manufacturer's protocol for the AB-PAS stain kit. Microscopic images of the stained sections were captured using an Olympus BX46 microscope. The number of goblet cells was counted in the crypts from multiple fields, as specified in the study protocol.

### Immunohistochemistry (IHC)

Immunohistochemical staining was performed using deparaffinized and rehydrated tissue sections. Endogenous peroxidase activity was quenched using hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) and non-specific antibody binding sites were blocked with bovine serum albumin (BSA) on the tissue sections. Tissue sections were incubated with the primary antibody overnight at 4 °C. The following day, the slides were incubated with the secondary antibody, followed by diaminobenzidine (DAB) staining.

### Western blot analysis (WB)

Proteins were extracted from colon tissue, resolved on a gel, transferred onto a polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) membrane, followed by blocking of nonspecific antibody binding sites on the PVDF membrane. The membrane was incubated with a primary antibody to bind to a specific antigen, washed, and incubated with a horse radish peroxidase-conjugated secondary antibody to specifically detect the primary antibody. A horse radish peroxidase-specific substrate was added to detect signal via chemifluorescence. Protein expression was quantified via a digital imaging scanner.

### Quantitative real-time polymerase chain reaction analysis (RT-qPCR)

RNA should be extracted using an RNA extraction kit. Reverse transcription should be performed to transcribe RNA into complementary DNA (cDNA). To prepare the qPCR reaction system, cDNA, primers (used to amplify the target RNA sequence), and probes (used to detect PCR reaction products) should be mixed. A thermal cycling PCR machine should be used to conduct multiple cycles of qPCR reaction, and the fluorescence data should be analyzed using the 2−ΔΔCT method to determine the expression level of the target RNA sequence.

### 16S rRNA gene amplification and sequencing

High-throughput 16S rDNA sequencing was used to detect various bacterial species and investigate alterations in gut microbiota. Total genomic DNA was isolated from fecal samples using commercially available kits. The purity and concentration of the DNA were assessed using a NanoDrop ND-1000 spectrophotometer and by agarose gel electrophoresis. A PCR amplification regimen was used to amplify the target region. The 468 base pairs V3-V4 region of the bacterial 16S rRNA gene, known for its high variability, was specifically selected for detecting various bacterial species via high-throughput rDNA sequencing [28], [29], and used primers designed for this purpose. The sequencing results were subsequently validated following standardized guidelines for library construction on the Illumina MiSeq instrument platform [30].

### Fecal metabolomics analysis

A Waters 2777c ultra-performance liquid chromatography (UPLC) instrument (Waters, USA) connected in-series to a Q Exactive HF benchtop mass spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA) was used for the separation and detection of metabolites. The chromatographic regimen was as follows. Chromatographic separation was performed on a Waters ACQUITY UPLC BEH C18 column (1.7 μm, 2.1 mm × 100 mm, Waters, USA), and the column temperature was maintained at 45 °C. The mobile phase consisted of 0.1 % formic acid (A) and acetonitrile (B) in the positive and negative modes, respectively, while the mobile phase consisted of 10 mM ammonium formate (A) and acetonitrile (B), respectively. The gradient conditions were as follows: 0–1 min, 2 % B; 1–9 min, 2 %-98 % B; 9–12 min, 98 % B; 12–12.1 min, 98 % B to 2 % B; and 12.1–15 min, 2 % B. The flow rate was 0.35 mL/min and the injection volume was 5 μL. Mass spectrometry conditions were as follows. Primary and secondary mass spectrometry data were acquired by employing the Q Exactive HF instrument (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA). The full scan range was 70–1050 m/z with a resolution of 120,000, and the automatic gain control (AGC) target for MS acquisitions was set to 3e6 with a maximum ion injection time of 100 ms. The top three precursors were selected for subsequent MS/MS fragmentation with a maximum ion injection time of 50 ms and a resolution of 30000, while the AGC was 1e5. The stepped normalized collision energies were set as 20, 40, and 60 eV. After importing the offline mass spectrometry data into compound discoverer 3.3 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA) and analyzing the mass spectrometry data in combination with bmdb (BGI metabolome database), mzcloud database, and chemspider online database, a data matrix containing information such as metabolite peak area and identification results was obtained. MetaX software was used to further preprocess the exported results, yielding compounds and quantitative values suitable for formal analysis [31].

### RNA sequencing analysis

High-throughput RNA sequencing was designed to study gene expression patterns through the sequencing and alignment analysis of transcripts to provide precise digital expression profiling. A specific quantity of RNA sample was carefully manipulated to unravel its secondary structure. Oligo (dT) magnetic beads were used for the selective enrichment of mRNA that was subsequently fragmented to enable first-strand cDNA synthesis. A meticulously designed two-strand synthesis reaction system was used, and the reaction protocol was precisely set to synthesize the second-strand cDNA and facilitate the linkage of the joint to the cDNA molecules. A well-designed PCR system coupled with an optimized reaction protocol was used to amplify the resulting products. After the PCR products were denatured into single strands, a cyclization reaction system was used, and the reaction procedure was meticulously designed to obtain a single-stranded circular product while effectively digesting the unused linear DNA molecules. Raw data acquired from high-throughput RNA sequencing were carefully filtered to generate high-quality data. Subsequently, a sophisticated multi-omics data mining system was used to conduct comprehensive data analysis, mapping, and mining [32], [33].

### Viability assay of a. Muciniphila

A. muciniphila was cultivated in sterilized thioglycolate medium tube, and treated with Rh4 at the final concentration of 50 µM (L-Rh4) and 100 µM (H-Rh4). Then, the OD600 value was recorded after co-cultivation at different time-course using microplate reader.

A. Muciniphila and CRC cell coculture

The bacterial species A. muciniphila was cultivated in a liquid thioglycollate medium at 37 °C within anaerobic gas-producing bags. The bacteria were passaged at a 3 % inoculation rate, their vitality met the experimental requirements after three generations, and they were cultured continuously for experimental use. HCT116 and CT26 cells were seeded overnight in sterile 96-well plates. Subsequently, the cell culture medium was replaced with a medium containing 10 % A. muciniphila bacteria for co-cultivation experiments. At 24, 48, and 72 h, the culture medium containing bacteria was replaced with normal culture medium. Spectrophotometric measurements were made using the CCK-8 assay to quantify HCT116 and CT26 cell viability.

### Cell proliferation analysis

For the determination of cell viability, HCT116 and CT26 cells were inoculated in a 96-well plate and 100 µM Rh4 or 600 μM UDCA was added. Subsequently, CCK-8 was utilized to evaluate cell viability, following the instructions provided by the manufacturer.

### Statistical analysis

Statistical comparisons between the two groups were performed utilizing either the parametric Student's t-test or the non-parametric Mann-Whitney U test, depending on the distribution of the data. For analyses involving multiple groups, the Kruskal-Wallis test or one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used. All statistical analyses were performed using the GraphPad Prism 9 (GraphPad, San Diego, CA, USA) or R Software. Statistical significance was defined as a P-value < 0.05.

Linear discriminant analysis effect size (LEfSe) also showed that A. muciniphila and Verrucomicrobiaceae were the marker bacteria of the Rh4 treatment group (Fig. 6C). Rh4 and Rh4_FMT treatment groups together showed a significant up-regulation of probiotics, including A. muciniphila, Barnesiella_intestinihominis, Bifidobacterium_longum (Fig. 6D-F), and the elimination of harmful bacteria such as Desulfovibrio_vietnamensis (Fig. 6G), of which A. muciniphila was the most significant. A. muciniphila has been identified as the next generation of probiotics. Based on this, we inferred that A. muciniphila was a key functional bacterium for Rh4 to inhibit CRC. Interestingly, our results showed that Rh4 significantly increased the viability of A. muciniphila in vitro (Fig. 6H). Co-culture experiments of A. muciniphila with CRC cells revealed that A. muciniphila could significantly inhibit the proliferation of HCT116 and CT26 cells, and Rh4 could enhance the anti-oncogenic ability of A. muciniphila (Fig. 6I-J). More importantly, both A. muciniphila and Rh4 did not affect the growth of normal cells in NCM460 normal colon epithelial cells (Fig. 6K). In summary, these collective findings affirm that A. muciniphila is a pivotal functional strain within the Rh4-modified gut microbiota, contributing to the inhibition of CRC.

### UDCA acts as the primary mediator for Rh4 in inhibiting colon tumorigenesis by regulating a. Muciniphila.

Given that gut bacteria typically influence host health and disease progression through metabolites, gut microbial metabolites serve as intermediaries for communication between gut microbiota and the host. Untargeted metabolomic analysis was employed to investigate the impact of Rh4 on the gut microbial metabolites of CRC mice. The score plot of PLS-DA plot demonstrated distinct separation between the Normal group, Model group and the Rh4 group (Fig. 7A and Fig. S10A). Permutation tests conducted on the OPLS-DA model confirmed its reliability (Fig. S10B). Subsequently, volcano plot and heatmap clustering analyses were conducted on the differential metabolite expression levels. Compared to the Model group, Rh4 notably upregulated beneficial metabolites including UCDA, taurodeoxycholic acid (TUDCA), 7-ketolithocholic acid, and L-Isoleucine (Fig. S10C-D). Conversely, significant downregulation was observed in metabolites like cholic acid (CA), deoxycholic acid (DCA), 2,6-pyridinedicarboxylic acid, and myristoleic acid (Fig. S10C-D). Furthermore, we observed that the metabolites significantly modulated by Rh4 and Rh4_FMT were predominantly related to BAs (Fig. 7B-C). Moreover, based on KEGG enrichment analysis, it was revealed that the primary pathway regulated by Rh4 and Rh4_FMT were the bile acid biosynthesis pathway (Fig. 7D-E). Notably, BAs are closely associated with CRC, while UDCA has been approved as a standard therapy for primary biliary cholangitis and has shown potential benefits in improving growth impairment in patients with IBD in previous studies [39]. In our research findings, we also observed a significant upregulation of UDCA by Rh4 and Rh4_FMT, along with a downregulation of the harmful CA (Fig. 7F-H). Therefore, we tentatively conclude that UDCA might be a primary mediator through which Rh4 regulates gut microbiota to inhibit CRC. Next, we explored the relationship between the key functional bacterium A. muciniphila and the primary mediator UDCA. A. muciniphila demonstrated a strong positive correlation with UDCA (Fig. 7I). When cultivated A. muciniphila in a culture medium containing 1 g/L CDCA, A. muciniphila promoted the conversion of chenodeoxycholic acid (CDCA) to UDCA by increasing the activity of 7α-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase (7α-HSDH). Moreover, the addition of Rh4 further enhanced the activity of 7α-HSDH, facilitating the conversion of primary BAs to secondary BAs (Fig. 7J). Furthermore, targeting BAs in the feces of mice supplemented with A. muciniphila revealed that A. muciniphila treatment increased the levels of UDCA, while reducing the levels of harmful DCA and lithocholic acid (LCA) (Fig. 7K). Subsequently, the results of co-culture between UDCA and CRC cells demonstrated that the proliferation and colony formation of CT26 and HCT116 in vitro were inhibited by UDCA, effectively impeding tumor cell growth (Fig. 8A-C). Similarly, UDCA also displayed the capacity to suppress colon tumor growth in vivo, thereby reducing the tumor burden in mice (Fig. 8D-F). Moreover, immunofluorescence analysis targeting CD8 + T cells revealed that treatment with Rh4 and UDCA can activate CD8 + T cell anti-tumor immune responses, with UDCA treatment exhibiting a stronger anti-tumor immune response (Fig. S11). These findings collectively confirm the significant role of UDCA for Rh4 in inhibiting CRC by modulating A. muciniphila.

### UDCA inhibits the occurrence of colon tumors through the FXR-TLR4-NF-κB signaling pathway

Transcriptome sequencing was performed to explore the anticancer mechanism of UDCA regulated by Rh4. Firstly, Rh4 treatment significantly modulated major signaling pathways associated with cancer, particularly the TLR signaling pathway, NF-κB signaling pathway and bile secretion pathways (Fig. 8G-I). Furthermore, quantitative analysis of differentially expressed proteins within the significantly enriched signaling pathways identified prominent upregulation of TLR4 protein, as well as excessive phosphorylation of P65 in the Model mice. However, supplementation with Rh4 and Rh4_FMT markedly inhibited the expression of TLR4 protein and reinstated the phosphorylation of P65 to their normal levels (Fig. S12). Given that the farnesoid X receptor (FXR) serves as a nuclear receptor of BAs, mounting evidence supports the pivotal role of FXR in human tumorigenesis [40]. Our results showed that Rh4 and Rh4_FMT could activate FXR receptor and inhibit TLR4-NF-κB signaling pathway (Fig. S12). Intriguingly, our findings revealed that UDCA treatment also upregulated FXR and suppressed the protein expression of TLR4 and inhibited the excessive phosphorylation of P65 (Fig. 8J-K). In summary, our results validated that UDCA serves as the crucial mediator for Rh4 in suppressing colon carcinogenesis by modulating A. muciniphila. Furthermore, UDCA exerted its inhibitory effect by activating FXR to suppress the TLR4 signaling pathway, thus inhibiting CRC.

### Discussion

CRC is a common cancer of the digestive tract that poses a serious threat to human health. Increasing clinical and experimental evidence indicates that gut microbiota play a significant role in the pathogenesis of CRC [41]. Manipulating the gut microbiota is considered a highly promising strategy for treatment of CRC. While we have previously demonstrated that Rh4 could regulate gut microbiota imbalance and inhibit the growth of CRC cells [24], [42], its mechanism is still not completely understood. To bridge this knowledge gap, we adopted a systematic approach to explore the interplay between Rh4 and gut microbiota in the prevention and treatment of CRC.

In our study, we employed a CRC model created using AOM/DSS and conducted conventional mouse experiments, antibiotic treatment, and FMT experiments. Ultimately, we conclusively demonstrated that Rh4 significantly reduced tumor burden and facilitated the restoration of intestinal barrier function in a microbiota-dependent manner, thereby effectively suppressing the development of CRC. When the mucosal barrier is breached, commensal microbes and pathogens can invade the mucus layer, making the host more susceptible to developing CRC [43], [44]. Our results showed that the gut barrier function was impaired in the Model group mice, while E-cadherin and Claudin-1 protein expression was also reduced. Both Rh4 and Rh4_FMT treatments elevated the expression of proteins associated with barrier function, underscoring their beneficial influence on intestinal barrier function. However in germ-free mice, although antibiotics reduced the tumor burden [37], Rh4 failed to further suppress disease malignancy. These findings further emphasize that Rh4 inhibits CRC and facilitates the restoration of the intestinal barrier function in a gut microbiota-dependent manner.

Given that gut microbiota have emerged as key players in the mechanism of CRC, we analyzed the influence of Rh4 on gut microbiota and the impact of gut microbiota on Rh4-mediated CRC inhibition. Our results showed that Rh4 counteracts gut dysbiosis. Notably, Rh4 increased the abundance of commensal probiotics such as A. muciniphila, Bifidobacterium_longum, Odoribacter splanchnicus, and Barnesiella intestinihominis. On the other hand, potential pathogenic species, such as Alistipes, Allobacullum, Rikenellaceae, Desulfovibrionaceae, and Oscillibacte were significantly decreased with Rh4. More importantly, Rh4 and Rh4_FMT showed similar results with respect to the regulation of the gut microbiota, with particular emphasis on the enrichment of A. muciniphila. Emerging evidence suggested that probiotics might be a promising manipulator to restore intestinal barrier function, balance immune responses, alleviate intestinal inflammation, and even mitigate CRC [45]. Functional studies conducted on significantly upregulated A. muciniphila suggested that A. muciniphila could significantly inhibit the growth of CRC cells and Rh4 could enhance its anti-cancer ability. A. muciniphila has emerged as a promising next-generation probiotic with potential clinical applications, especially in the prevention and treatment of conditions such as diabetes, obesity, and cancer [46], [47], [48]. Recent studies confirmed that A. muciniphila stimulated the secretion of specific cytokines by human immune cells through its membrane phospholipids. Furthermore, it modulated the activation threshold of dendritic cells and regulated subsequent immune responses [49]. Moreover, the secretion of Amuc_2172 by A. muciniphila was demonstrated to reshape the tumor microenvironment by stimulating the production of heat shock proteins and enhancing the immune response mediated by cytotoxic T lymphocytes [50]. Besides, Barnesiella intestinihominis could stimulate the accumulation of CD8+ T cells and Th1 cells, thus promoting the induction of anticancer immunity by cyclophosphamide [51]. Odoribacter splanchnicus induced the differentiation of Th17 cells in both normal and germ-free mice, and exhibited a significant protective effect against colitis and CRC [52]. In future studies, we plan to investigate these functions. Collectively, Rh4 suppressed CRC, at least in part, by increasing the abundance of probiotics and depleting potential CRC pathogens, with particular emphasis on the enrichment of A. muciniphila.

Accumulating evidence suggests that gut microbiota-mediated metabolites are key signaling molecules in host-microbial cross-talk. For instance, BAs are pivotal constituents of the gastrointestinal tract, bridging the connection between the gut microbiota and hepatic and intestinal metabolism [53], [54]. Early studies demonstrated a positive correlation between a high-fat diet and fecal BA levels; accordingly, high levels of BA were detected in the feces of patients with CRC and healthy individuals who were at a high risk for CRC [7], [55]. In our study, feces metabolomics data showed that both Rh4 and Rh4_FMT significantly regulated the BA biosynthesis pathway and up-regulated the beneficial secondary BAs, such as UDCA and TUDCA. Spearman correlation analysis showed that UDCA levels were strongly positively correlated with A.muciniphila abundance. Previous studies have shown that A.muciniphila can promote the conversion of primary bile acid CDCA into taurochenodeoxycholic acid (TCDCA) by liver cells, which can inhibit the expression of pro-inflammatory factors through the bile acid receptor TGR5, thereby alleviating systemic inflammatory response[56]. In our study, we also confirmed the effect of A.muciniphila on BA metabolism. In vitro studies we have confirmed that A.muciniphila can promote the conversion of CDCA to UDCA by increasing the activity of 7α-HSDH, thereby increasing the level of UDCA. As an important secondary BA, UDCA serves predominantly as an anticholestatic agent and represents a primary therapeutic choice for individuals with autoimmune biliary disorders [57], [12], [58]. Here, we demonstrated that UDCA directly inhibits CRC both in vitro and in vivo. In summary, these results indicate that Rh4 regulates BA metabolism in a gut microbiota-dependent manner, with particular emphasis on the regulation of the beneficial UDCA, thus inhibiting CRC.

In addition, UDCA is an FXR agonist, and studies have demonstrated that the FXR receptor assumes the role of a key sentinel for BA homeostasis and FXR influences the development of CRC [59], [60]. In clinical studies, decreased expression of FXR has been observed in patients with CRC, and in these patients, the level of FXR in the tumor was negatively correlated with the severity of CRC [61]. In the context of the AOM/DSS-induced inflammation-associated CRC murine model, it has been demonstrated that treatment with the FXR agonist effectively diminishes the incidence of colonic mucosal ulcers and adenocarcinoma occurrences [62]. Suppression of FXR activity further exacerbated CRC and FXR agonist GW4064 could enhance anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy of CRC [63]. Consistent with these earlier findings, our results also showed that Rh4 regulated UDCA and activated FXR to play a role in tumor suppression. Moreover, studies in IBD have confirmed that FXR activity is inhibited by NF-κB overexpression, signifying the involvement of NF-κB in the regulation of FXR activity [64]. FXR also inhibited the onset of intrahepatic inflammation by suppressing the STAT3 and NF-κB inflammatory pathways in hepatocytes, thereby inhibiting hepatocellular carcinoma [65]. In our results, transcriptome data and WB data showed that Rh4 significantly regulated the inflammation-related pathways, including the TLR and the NF-κB signaling pathway. These findings indicate that Rh4 modulates bile acid metabolism in a gut microbiota-dependent manner, promoting the generation of beneficial UDCA. It further activates FXR, regulates the TLR4 signaling pathway associated with inflammation, facilitates the repair of intestinal barrier function, and inhibits CRC.

### Conclusions

In summary, our findings show that Rh4 exerts an inhibitory effect on CRC by gut microbiota-mediated modulation of UDCA. UDCA further activates FXR and modulates the TLR4-NF-κB signaling pathway, effectively facilitating repair of intestinal barrier damage and reducing the expression of inflammatory factors, thereby inhibiting CRC. Our study emphasizes that Rh4 can act as a regulator of gut microbiota, exerting an inhibitory effect on CRC in a gut microbiota-dependent manner. These findings offer novel insights for the development of new natural drugs for the prevention and treatment of CRC.
