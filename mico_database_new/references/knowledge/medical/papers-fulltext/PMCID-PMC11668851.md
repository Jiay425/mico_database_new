## Abstract

AbstractEvidence suggests that complex interactions among the gut microbiome, metabolic abnormalities, and brain have important etiological and therapeutic implications in major depressive disorder (MDD). However, the influence of microbiome-gut-brain cross-talk on cognitive impairment in MDD remains poorly characterized. We performed serum metabolomic profiling on 104 patients with MDD and 77 healthy controls (HCs), and also performed fecal metagenomic sequencing on a subset of these individuals, including 79 MDD patients and 60 HCs. The findings were validated in a separate cohort that included 40 patients with MDD and 40 HCs using serum-targeted metabolomics. Abnormal bile acid metabolism was observed in patients with MDD, which is related to cognitive dysfunction. The following gut microbiota corresponded to changes in bile acid metabolism and enzyme activities involved in the bile acid metabolic pathway, including Lachnospiraceae (Blautia_massiliensis, Anaerostipes_hadrus, Dorea_formicigenerans, and Fusicatenibacter_saccharivorans), Ruminococcaceae (Ruminococcus_bromii, Flavonifractor_plautii, and Ruthenibacterium_lactatiformans), and Escherichia_coli. Furthermore, a combinatorial marker classifier that robustly differentiated patients with MDD from HCs was identified. In conclusion, this study provides insights into the gut-brain interactions in the cognitive phenotype of MDD, indicating a potential therapeutic strategy for MDD-associated cognitive impairment by targeting the gut microbiota and bile acid metabolism.Subject terms: Depression, Psychiatric disorders

### Introduction

Major depressive disorder (MDD) is a highly prevalent and recurrent condition, impacting over 350 million individuals globally [1]. Besides emotional symptoms, cognitive symptoms are also recognized as one of the core clinical manifestations of MDD and are also associated with a poor response to current antidepressant treatment [2]. Mood improvements do not necessarily translate into functional improvements. Deficits in executive function and attention might continue even when the emotional symptoms are completely alleviated [3]. Cognitive deficits that persist following the remission of depressive symptoms have been demonstrated to hinder the complete restoration of functional abilities, thereby resulting in increases in societal expenses allocated to the treatment of MDD [4].

Dysbiosis in the composition and functionality of gut microbiota may perturb immunological and metabolic pathways, contributing to the onset of psychiatric disorders, including MDD [5, 6]. Fecal microbiota transplantation using microbiota from MDD patients has been shown to induce depression-like behavior in mice, whereas probiotic supplementation can modify depression-like behavior in animal experiments [7, 8]. Population studies have also found that supplementation with probiotics, exercise, and dietary modifications can modulate the gut microbiota and potentially demonstrate antidepressant effects [9, 10]. In addition, other studies have demonstrated the causal role of the gut microbiome on cognition [11–13]. Gut microbial dysbiosis contributes to the development of various neurodegenerative disorders, which are often associated with cognitive impairment [14]. Studies have shown that probiotic supplementation can improve cognitive impairment in patients with MDD and Alzheimer’s disease (AD) [15, 16]. This dysfunction of microbiota-gut-brain axis is associated with various metabolic changes, involving the disruption of amino acid, energy, bile acid, carbohydrate, and lipid metabolism [5, 17–19]. Microbial metabolites are the connection and mediators between the gut and the brain; specifically, short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), secondary bile acids (BAs), fatty acid, and tryptophan play a critical role in metabolism. Some metabolites of the tryptophan pathway and SCFAs are depleted in patients with MDD [17, 20, 21]. Moreover, certain metabolites possess neuroprotective potential, including tauroursodeoxycholic acid (TUDCA), which could be involved in the prevention of AD [22, 23]. Upregulated microbiota-derived indoles can restore synaptic plasticity, and promote cognitive ability in an AD mouse model [24]. Microbiota and microbiota-derived metabolites modulate emotional and cognitive functions by regulating the permeability of the blood-brain barrier, cerebral energy homeostasis, and synaptic signaling [25].

The mechanism of cognitive impairment in MDD involves oxidative stress, neuroactive metabolites transmission, and other signaling pathways [26]. However, as of yet, there has been insufficient investigation into the impact of gut microbiota on cognitive function in patients with MDD. Most studies summarizing the evidence on the role of gut microbes and its metabolites in the development of psychiatric disorders, including MDD, do not refer to “cognition impairment” which is essential for the rehabilitation of social function [27, 28]. Two recent studies attempted to elucidate this matter using the 16S rRNA sequencing method; however, the sample sizes were limited and the studies primarily concentrated on the alterations in single “omics”, such as focusing on the analysis of gut microbiota composition while overlooking microbiota-derived metabolites, which serve as a critical conduit for the “cross-talk” between the brain and the gut microbiota [29, 30].

This study aims to address this knowledge gap. We conducted a multi-omics analysis of the gut microbiota and serum metabolome using data from 104 patients with MDD and 77 healthy controls (HCs). The changes in metabolomics were validated in another cohort that included 40 patents with MDD and 40 HCs. The multi-omics approach has the advantage of using multidimensional data which benefits the identification of biomarkers of cognitive deficits in MDD. The current study contributes to our understanding of the role of the gut-brain axis in the pathogenesis of cognitive impairment in MDD. The research also offers a crucial resource for future biomarker studies and the creation of innovative intervention methods for preventing and treating cognitive dysfunction in MDD.

### Cognitive functioning assessment

Cognitive function was evaluated utilizing the THINC-Integrated Tool (THINC-it) on a tablet, which consists of a series digitized cognitive tests. These tests included one subjective test known as Perceived Deficits Questionnaire for Depression (PDQ-5-D), and four objective tests: the Identification Task (IDN) using the Choice Reaction Time paradigm (Spotter), which reflects attention and executive function, the One-Back Test (Symbol Check), which reflects working memory, executive function, and attention, the Digit Symbol Substitution Test (Codebreaker), which evaluates executive function, processing speed and attention, and the Trail Making Test (Trails) can evaluate executive functions [31]. THINC-it objective cognition was calculated as the average of the Z-scores from the four objective tests, while composite cognition was determined by the average of all five tests in the THINC-it battery. The Z-scores for the PDQ-5-D, Spotter, and Trials showed a positive correlation with the severity of cognitive impairment. To adjust for this, the Z-scores for PDQ-5-D, Spotter, and Trails were multiplied by -1 to reverse the scoring (i.e., negative scores were made positive, and vice versa), with higher scores indicating better cognitive performance.

### Metagenomic sequencing and functional analysis

Fecal samples were collected from 139 participants (79 patients with MDD and 60 HCs) in cohort 1. These biological samples are stored in the Biobank of the First Affiliated Hospital of Xi’an Jiaotong University. DNA was extracted from the stool samples using the Magbeads Fast DNA® kit (MP Biomedicals, Shanghai, China). The enrichment of microbial gene functions was analyzed using the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) database to identify differences [32]. Additional information regarding metagenomic sequencing and functional analysis is provided in the Supplementary Materials.

### Untargeted metabolomics and targeted amino acid and bile acid profiling

Blood samples were collected from 104 patients with MDD and 77 HC individuals in a fasting state from 6 A.M. to 7 A.M., after centrifugation, the serum samples were stored at −80 °C in the Biobank of the First Affiliated Hospital of Xi’an Jiaotong University. Untargeted metabolomic analysis was performed using liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS). Targeted validation of amino and BAs was performed in another cohort of 40 patients with MDD and 40 HCs. All standards were acquired from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO, USA) and Zhenzhun. Targeted LC-MS/MS analysis was carried using a Shimadzu LC system coupled to a triple quadrupole mass spectrometer (QTRAP® 6500+, ABSciex). Data acquisition was conducted using Analyst 1.6.3 software (Sciex), and metabolite quantification was facilitated using Multiquant 3.0.3 software (Sciex). The specifics are provided in Supplementary Materials.

### Statistical analysis

All analyses were performed using SPSS (version 25) and R software (R-4.3.1). Continuous variables were compared using the Student’s t-test and Wilcoxon’s rank sum-test. Categorical data were analyzed using the chi-square or Fisher’s exact tests. Analysis of Covariance (ANCOVA) was used to compare cognitive function between the two groups.

The differential abundance of gut microbes was tested by Wilcoxon’s rank-sum test between MDD patients and HCs, and the Benjamini-Hochberg method (with the threshold set at <0.05) was used to adjust the P-values. Beta diversity was computed using both the Bray-Curtis distance and the Jaccard distance metric, and the results were visualized through principal coordinates analysis (PCoA). To assess the influence of clinical measures, we performed PERMANOVA analysis of the species abundance data, based on a Bray–Curtis dissimilarity metric and 999 permutations, was performed using the vegan package of R (version 3.5.0). The Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA) Effect Size (LEfSe) method was employed to identify specific species that characterized the MDD and HC groups [33].

VIP values were derived from the Orthogonal Partial Least Squares-Discriminant Analysis (OPLS-DA) and were visualized using the R package MetaboAnalystR. The different metabolites were assessed using Student’s t-test, and the false discovery rate (FDR) was adjusted using the Benjamini–Hochberg method. Differential metabolites were determined based on VIP > 1 and FDR < 0.05.

The relationship between the gut microbiota, serum metabolites, and cognitive function was conducted via Co-inertia analysis (CIA). The random 4.6-12 package was used to construct a classifier to discriminate between MDD and HCs based on a random forest model with the metabolites (bile acid and gut-derived neurotransmitter-like metabolites) and metagenomic species (species with LDA > 2.5 and P-value < 0.05), respectively. The variables with the highest predictive power were identified as those that yielded the maximum Area Under the Curve (AUC), utilizing the pROC package in R. Lasso penalized regression (LASSO) is a machine-learning method. Feature selection was implemented by adding an L1 regularization term to the loss function. We used LASSO to determine BAs that were related to cognitive function using the R package glmnet (version 2.0-13). The associations between gut microbiota, BAs, and cognitive performance were analyzed using Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient and visualized via the Cytoscape software (version 3.10.0).

### Clinical characteristics and data collection

In the MDD group from cohort 1, 93 individuals were medication-naïve, while 11 had intermittently used low-dose antidepressants for a duration of no more than 1 week (five with sertraline and six with escitalopram). In cohort 2, all patients with MDD were medication-naïve. Except for education level, there was no significant difference between the MDD and HCs groups from the two cohorts in terms of demographic data (i.e., age, sex, BMI, blood pressure, dietary habits, drinking, and smoking history; Supplementary Table 1, P-value < 0.05). In Cohort 1, 174 individuals completed the THINC-it test (101 patients with MDD and 73 HCs), and all participants in Cohort 2 finished the THINC-it test. Analysis of covariance for both cohorts showed that the MDD group had lower Z-scores than HCs on the Spotter, Codebreaker, Trials, PDQ-5-D, THINC-it Objective Cognition, and THINC-it Composite Cognition tests (Supplementary Table 2, P-value < 0.05).

### Abnormal composition and function of gut microbiota in patients with MDD

Metagenome shotgun sequencing was employed to analyze the gut microbiota in 139 fecal samples, yielding an average of 163.74 million high-quality reads per sample (Supplementary Table 3). Among the MDD patients, five had a history of intermittent low-dose medication use. This dosage and frequency were considered not to have an impact on the composition of the gut microbiota [18]. Analysis of alpha-diversity indices revealed no significant disparities between the MDD and HC groups (Fig. 2A). PCoA showed that there were significant differences between the two groups (Fig. 2B and Supplementary Fig. 1A). PERMANOVA was applied to evaluate the impact of each clinical measure on the variations in species abundance. Disorder status and the PHQ-9 scores were significantly correlated with differences between microbial species. Additionally, sex exerted a moderate but significant effect on gut microbiome changes in patients with MDD (Supplementary Table 4). A total of 123 differential species were detected, 67 of which were enriched in patients with MDD, and 56 were depleted. Species enriched in patients with MDD mainly belonged to the Lachnospiraceae, Ruminococcaceae, Streptococcaceae, Lactobacillaceae, Clostridiaceae, Veillonellaceae, Actinomycetaceae, and Clostridia_unclassified families. Depleted species in the MDD group mainly belonged to Lachnospiraceae, Clostridiaceae, Ruminococcaceae, Peptostreptococcaceae, Erysipelotrichaceae, Eubacteriaceae, and Bacteroidales_unclassified families (Supplementary Fig. 1B and Supplementary Table 5, P-value < 0.05). LDA was used to estimate the impact of species abundance on these differences. Differences in the relative abundance of species with LDA > 2.5 and P-value < 0.05 are shown in Fig. 2C and Supplementary Fig. 2.

Functional analyses of the microbiome showed that changes in pathways in patients with MDD were mainly involved in primary bile acid biosynthesis, secondary bile acid biosynthesis, amino acid metabolism (e.g., tryptophan metabolism, lysine degradation, and histidine metabolism), glutathione metabolism, arachidonic acid metabolism, and fatty acid metabolism (Fig. 2D and Supplementary Table 6). This suggests that changes in the gut microbiome are related to metabolic activity in patients with MDD. Among the above differential pathways, fatty acid biosynthesis and metabolism, bile acid biosynthesis, tryptophan metabolism, glutathione metabolism, and other amino acid metabolic pathways were correlated with clinical symptoms and cognitive function in MDD (Supplementary Fig. 3). The above results suggest that it is essential to explore the influence of the gut microbiota on metabolic processes in conjunction with metabolomics. Therefore, we used untargeted LC-MS to analyze changes in the serum metabolic profile and its response to microbiota disturbances in patients with MDD.

### Aberrant serum metabolic characteristic in patients with MDD

Using untargeted LC-MS, OPLS-DA revealed that the overall serum profiles of patients with MDD were substantially distinct from those of HCs (Fig. 3A; Q2 = 0.903, P-value < 0.005). We identified 4147 metabolic features in 181 samples. A total of 1236 features were altered in MDD (310 were upregulated while 926 were downregulated; VIP > 1 and FDR < 0.05; Fig. 3B and Supplementary Table 7). To elucidate the potential roles of metabolites, we annotated their metabolic pathways using the KEGG database. The findings showed that patients with MDD displayed alterations in 147 metabolic pathways (Supplementary Table 8), including primary bile acid biosynthesis, cholesterol metabolism, steroid hormone biosynthesis, pantothenate and CoA biosynthesis, tryptophan metabolism, arachidonic acid metabolism, biosynthesis of cofactors, and other pathways of amino acid metabolism (Fig. 3C, E). Metabolites associated with steroid hormone biosynthesis, tryptophan metabolism, phenylalanine and tyrosine biosynthesis, biosynthesis of cofactors, and pantothenate and CoA biosynthesis predominantly decreased. In contrast, metabolites related to arachidonic acid metabolism, glutathione metabolism, and glycine, serine, and threonine metabolism were predominantly increased. We found that the primary BAs glycochenodeoxycholic acid (GCDCA) and taurochenodeoxycholic acid (TCDCA) were upregulated, taurocholic acid (TCA) was downregulated, and secondary BAs were generally downregulated, except glycolithocholic acid (GLCA), this aligns with the partial findings of gut microbial functional alterations (Fig. 2D and Fig. 3E). To further confirm the above changes in metabolic pathways, we conducted a targeted validation of serum amino acid and bile acid profiles in another cohort. The verification results further confirmed abnormal bile acid biosynthesis, tryptophan metabolism, and cholesterol metabolism in patients with MDD (Fig. 3D). In summary, these results suggest abnormal overall serum metabolic characteristics.

Increasing evidence suggests that dysregulation of the tryptophan metabolism may play a role in various psychiatric disorders, such as schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and MDD [17, 27, 34]. Echoing the above studies, we found that the metabolites involved in the tryptophan pathway were significantly reduced (e.g., L-formylkynurenine, indole, indole-3-acetamide, and 5-hydroxy-L-tryptophan). We also found that pantothenic acid, a B vitamin, was reduced, whereas L-glutamic acid, an excitatory neurotransmitter, was elevated (Fig. 3F).

Several studies indicate that the gut-bile acid-host axis contributes to the development of MDD by modulating the immune system [35, 36]. Our results showed that GCDCA, TCDCA, and GLCA were upregulated in patients with MDD, whereas TCA, lithocholic acid (LCA), taurolithocholic acid (TLCA), glycoursodeoxycholic acid (GUDCA), and tauroursodeoxycholic acid (TUDCA) were downregulated (Fig. 3F). Based on the expanded sample size, we confirmed abnormal bile acid metabolism in patients with MDD.

Various research efforts have demonstrated that gut microbiota can synthesize microbial-host isozymes, potentially contributing to the regulation of host well-being and disease states [37, 38]. We used metagenomic data from HUMAnN to identify the enzymes involved in metabolism. Reaction steps, metabolites, enzymes involved and enzyme-producing gut microbiota of tryptophan metabolism, citrate cycle (TCA cycle), and pantothenate and CoA biosynthesis are shown in Supplementary Fig. 4 and Supplementary Fig. 5. Changes were observed in both metabolites and enzymes involved in these pathways. These results partly explain the mechanism by which gut microbiota mediates metabolic abnormalities in MDD.

### Abnormal serum metabolome correlates with microbiota alterations in MDD

Based on the above findings, we performed CIA analysis of the omics datasets to assess the interplay between the gut microbiome and serum metabolomics. The analysis revealed significant associations between gut microorganisms and serum metabolites (Fig. 4A). Primary BAs undergo extensive bacterial transformations in the intestine, producing secondary BAs. Diet-derived amino acids can also produce neurotransmitter-like metabolites in bacteria. Gut-derived metabolites play significant roles in the maintenance of host physiology and homeostasis. In addition, evidence points to the abnormality of bile acid and amino acid metabolism based on the results of gut microbial functional analysis, untargeted metabolic, and targeted bile acid profiling. We analyzed the contributions of microbes to BAs and neurotransmitter-like metabolites based on correlation analysis and a random forest model. The metabolites were correlated with the abundance of species and the results suggested that GLCA and GUDCA were widely related to gut microbes, with the explained variance of the random forest models of 11.21% and 10.93%, respectively (Fig. 4B and Supplementary Table 9). Phascolarctobacterium_faecium, Escherichia_coli, Flavonifractor_plautii, Enterocloster_aldensis, Veillonella_parvula, and Enterocloster_bolteae were strong positive predictors of GLCA, whereas Blautia_massiliensis, Faecalibacillus_intestinalis, Dorea_formicigenerans, Blautia_massiliensis, Ruminococcus_bromii, and Anaerobutyricum_hallii were negative predictors. Clostridiales_bacterium_KLE1615, Roseburia_faecis, Dorea_formicigenerans, Ruminococcus_bromii, Eubacterium_ventriosum, Eubacterium_ramulus, Ruminococcus_bicirculans, Anaerostipes_hadrus and Megamonas_funiformis were the major positive constituents contributing to GUDCA, major negative predictors were Bilophila_wadsworthia, Ruthenibacterium_lactatiformans, Flavonifractor_plautii, and Dysosmobacter_welbionis (Fig. 4B). Neurotransmitter-like metabolites were related to most of the microbes (Fig. 4C). In the random forest models, gut microbiome had a higher prediction of 5-hydroxy-L-tryptophan (Explained variation = 7.82%), indole-3-acetamide (Explained variation = 9.03%), and L-glutamic acid (Explained variation = 28.62%) (Fig. 4C and Supplementary Table 9). Clostridium_sp_NSJ_42, Roseburia_faecis, Dorea_longicatena, and Blautia_massiliensis were the major positive constituents of metabolites in the tryptophan pathway. We assessed the relationship between the relative abundance of species and its associated clinical phenotype. Notably, the species implicated in the generation of gut-derived metabolites exhibited a strong correlation with the clinical phenotypes of MDD (Supplementary Fig. 6). Based on these findings, we hypothesized that the microbiota could regulate gut-derived metabolites to affect MDD pathophysiology and that BAs are also important contributors.

A disease classifier was constructed based on gut-derived BAs, neurotransmitter-like metabolites, and the species discussed above using a random forest model to differentiate patients with MDD from HCs. In the model including BAs, the AUC was 0.88 when using only BAs; the AUC increased to 0.99 after combining microbiota. GLCA, TLCA, GUDCA, and TUDCA were the major contributors (Fig. 4D). In the model that included gut-derived neurotransmitter-like metabolites, the AUC was 0.93; after the addition of microbes, the AUC increased to 0.99 (Supplementary Fig. 7). Our findings suggest that microbes and gut-derived BAs may be potential diagnostic biomarkers of MDD and that the efficiency of diagnosis is comparable to that of widely known neurotransmitters.

### Gut microbes mediated abnormal bile acid metabolic pathway in MDD

Primary BAs undergo conversion to secondary BAs via dehydroxylation, a process facilitated by the enzyme 7-alpha-dehydroxylase produced by gut bacteria. BAs interact with microorganisms each other. An imbalance of bile acid-microbiota can enhance inflammation and contribute to disease occurrence [39]. Given the abnormal bile acid profile mentioned above, HUMAnN was used to compare metagenomic data to identify and analyze the enzymes involved in bile acid metabolism. The processes of bile acid metabolism, along with the key enzymes implicated in these pathways, are summarized in Fig. 5A, which include bile salt hydrolase (BSH) (EC3.5.1.24), 7-alpha-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase (EC1.1.1.159) and 7-beta-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase (NADP(+)) (EC1.1.1.201) (Fig. 5A). We found the gene abundance of EC3.5.1.24 was decreased, whereas that of EC1.1.1.159 was elevated in patients with MDD (Fig. 5B).

BSH catalyzes the production of unconjugated BAs and regulates the homeostasis of the BA pool. A recent study conducted by Quinn et al. BSH can also catalyze the production of a variety of microbially conjugated BAs (MCBAs). These MCBAs hold the potential to influence both the gut microbiome and human health [40]. To explore the potential roles of specific bacterial species in bile acid pathway enzymes, we analyzed the major attributing species for gene-encoding enzymes. The results indicated that Bacteroides_uniformis, Blautia_obeum, Blautia_wexlerae, and Anaerostipes_hadrus were the four species that make major contributions to EC3.5.1.24 in MDD (Fig. 5C). The abundance of genes encoding EC3.5.1.24 from Anaerostipes_hadrus, Blautia_obeum, Blautia_wexlerae, Ruminococcus_torques, and Dorea_longicatena was lower in patients with MDD (Fig. 5D), which coincided with the decrease in the relative abundance of these species in patients with MDD, all of which belong to the Lachnospiraceae (Fig. 2C). In addition, we observed that the relative abundance of genes encoding EC1.1.1.159 major species, including Escherichia_coli, Klebsiella_oxytoca, and Klebsiella_pneumoniae, were elevated (Fig. 5E, F). In summary, these results suggest a mechanism by which gut microbes mediate abnormal bile acid metabolism in patients with MDD, partly by regulating enzymatic activity.

BAs regulate metabolism and immunity and maintain systemic homeostasis through the nuclear receptor (FXR), TGR5 (G-protein-coupled bile acid receptor), vitamin D receptor, and other receptors, which are not only auxiliary digestive factors, but also important endocrine regulatory factors [41, 42]. To explore the role of abnormal bile acid in peripheral inflammation in patients with MDD, we compared peripheral white blood cell ratios and found elevated mononuclear (MONO%) and lymphatic (LYMPH%) percentages, whereas neutrophil granulocytes (NEUT%) were decreased (Fig. 5G). We further examined the serum cytokines in 47 patients with MDD from cohort 1 and performed a correlation analysis with BAs. GUDCA was negatively correlated with IL-6, and GLCA was positively correlated with IL-17 in patients with MDD (Fig. 5H). This finding suggests that abnormal bile acid metabolism may be associated with changes in levels of inflammatory factors in patients with MDD.

### Gut microbes influenced bile acid metabolism promote cognitive impairment in MDD

We further explored the mechanisms underlying cognitive impairment in MDD, examining the functions of the gut microbiota and BAs. We evaluated the impact of serum metabolomics, gut microbiota, and cognition. CIA revealed a stronger association between the serum metabolome and cognition (Fig. 6A) compared to the relationship between gut microbiota and cognition (Fig. 6B), potentially indicating the impact of gut microbes on cerebral processes via a more complex pathway, including the immune, endocrine, and nervous systems [43]. To further explore the effect of abnormal bile acid profiles on cognitive impairment in patients with MDD, LASSO regression was used to screen BAs that significantly contributed to cognitive function. BAs were found to contribute to several dimensions of cognitive function in patients with MDD, after accounting for age, sex, BMI, and degree of education (Supplementary Table 10). The results showed that gut-derived secondary BAs, including GLCA, GUDCA, and TUDCA, were related to cognitive impairment in patients with MDD. GUDCA and TUDCA were positive contributors to THINC-it Objective Cognition and THINC-it Composite Cognition, whereas GLCA was a negative factor (Fig. 6C, D). Taking each test separately as a dependent variable, the results showed that GUDCA was a positive predictor for Spotter, Codebreaker, Trials, and PDQ-5-D, whereas GLCA was a negative factor for Spotter and Trials (Supplementary Fig. 8). This indicates that GLCA, GUDCA, and TUDCA are correlated with several cognitive dimensions. The results also showed that GCDCA, a conjugated primary BA, was negatively correlated with THINC-it Composite Cognition, Trials, and PQD-5-D (Fig. 6C and Supplementary Fig. 8).

In the interaction network of the gut microbiome, BAs, and cognitive function, the results showed that the gut microbiome was strongly related to GUDCA, TUDCA, and GLCA. Twenty-three species were related to GUDCA. Of these, most were from the Lachnospiraceae and Ruminococcaceae family. Eight species were linked to TUDCA. GLCA was related to seventeen species, which mainly included Lachnospiraceae (Fig. 6E and Supplementary Table 11). This suggests that cognitive impairment in patients with MDD may be similar to the pathological process of neurodegenerative disorders. Additional studies are required to validate these findings.

### Discussion

Cognitive symptoms in patients with MDD respond poorly to widely used drugs that primarily act on 5-Hydroxytryptamine (5-HT) and norepinephrine (NE) receptors, suggesting that there may be other mechanisms underlying cognitive impairment. These results demonstrate that bile acid metabolism is significantly abnormal in patients with MDD, which is linked to changes in the gut microbiome and cognitive impairment. This study’s multi-omics data have, for the first time, identified a network of interactions within the gut-bile acid-brain axis in MDD patients with cognitive impairment. These findings provide a new therapeutic target for cognitive symptoms in MDD and suggest that the development of future probiotics or drugs to target bile acid metabolic pathways may be beneficial for cognition in patients with MDD.

Previous studies on the gut microbiome in depression have mainly focused on the severity of disease symptoms. Two studies using the 16S rRNA sequencing method, both characterized by small sample sizes, discovered alterations in gut microbes among patients with depression, suggesting possible impacts on cognitive function [29, 30]. Notably, our study employed metagenomic sequencing to identify microorganisms that exhibit a strong correlation with MDD, including Blautia_massiliensis, Anaerostipes_hadrus, Dorea_formicigenerans, and Fusicatenibacter_saccharivorans belonging to Lachnospiraceae; Ruminococcus_bromii; Flavonifractor_plautii; and Ruthenibacterium_lactatiformans from Ruminococcaceae and Escherichia_coli. These species exhibited a significant correlation with cognitive symptoms in patients with MDD. Lachnospiraceae and Ruminococcaceae, known for their role in butyrate and serotonin production, are considered beneficial microbes and be reduced in abundance in individuals with MDD [44, 45]. Gavaging rats with Escherichia_coli resulted in decreased serum estradiol and testosterone levels and induced depression-like behaviors [46, 47]. These results support our findings. Additionally, microbial dysbiosis is significantly correlated with cognition. Studies on age-related gut microbes found that the microbiota undergoes extensive changes; Faecalibacterium, Bacteroidaceae, and Lachnospiraceae are relatively reduced with aging and cognitive decline [48]. Ruminococcaceae, Lachnospiraceae, and Lactobacillus were found to be decreased in patients with AD, while Escherichia increased at the genus level in both fecal and blood samples from patients with MCI and AD [49, 50]. Lachnospiraceae and Ruminococcaceae, known to produce SCFAs, are the main predictors of hyperphosphorylated tau in AD [51]. Changes in gut microbes may contribute to cognitive impairment in MDD and a similar pathological process may occur in the above diseases with cognitive dysfunction.

Communication occurs bidirectionally between gut microbes and the brain through multiple pathways [52]. Gut microbiota convert and metabolize both dietary and host-originated compounds, producing a wide array of metabolites that exert local and systemic impacts. These metabolites are likely critical initiators of communication along the gut-brain axis [53]. Our results revealed significant changes in the overall serum metabolic signatures of patients with MDD. Specifically, KEGG enrichment analysis revealed significant changes in bile acid metabolism, amino acid metabolism, steroid hormone biosynthesis, and pantothenate and CoA biosynthesis. Consistent with the findings of previous studies [17, 27], we observed that the majority of metabolites from the tryptophan pathway were downregulated. L-glutamic acid is an excitatory neurotransmitter, and its levels in report on depression are still contradictory [54, 55]. Our results showed an increase in L-glutamic acid levels, which is consistent with findings that indicated elevated levels of L-glutamate in the postmortem brain tissue from MDD patients [56]. Elevated glutamate levels may play a role in the development of MDD by functioning as agonists at N-methyl-d-aspartate (NMDA) receptor [57]. Ketamine, an NMDA antagonist, has been shown to exert rapid antidepressant effects [58]. Moreover, the neurotoxicity of excess glutamate has been demonstrated in neurodegenerative diseases [59, 60]. To further assess the relationship between the metabolites mentioned above and the gut microbiota, we used a random forest model to screen metabolite-associated gut microbes. L-Glutamic acid is widely related to most gut microbes. Clostridium_sp_NSJ_42, Roseburia_faecis, Dorea_longicatena, and Blautia_massiliensis were the major positive constituents of metabolites in the tryptophan pathway. These gut-derived metabolites have neurotransmitter-like functions and suggest that the regulation of gut microbes may be beneficial for neurotransmitter imbalance in patients with MDD. Our work contributes to the current evidence base by identifying the microbes and neurotransmitter-like metabolites related to MDD.

Beyond the metabolic pathways mentioned above, we observed significant abnormalities in bile acid metabolism. Compared with healthy controls, GCDCA, TCDCA, and GLCA were up-regulated, whereas TCA, LCA, TLCA, GUDCA, and TUDCA were downregulated in MDD. The results of random forest models showed that these BAs were widely correlated with gut microbes in patients with MDD, with major contributors from Lachnospiraceae, Ruminococcaceae, and Clostridiales_unclassified. Escherichia_coli was also a key contributor. Coincidentally, we found that these microbes promote human bile acid metabolism are significantly abnormal in patients with MDD. Oscillospiraceae, Ruminococcaceae, Lachnospiracea, and Clostridia induce secondary bile acid deficiency, which promotes an inflammatory state in the intestine [61, 62]. This indicates that gut dysbiosis may lead to disrupted bile acid metabolism and participate in the advancement of MDD. We distinguished patients with MDD from HCs based on the gut microbiota and bile acids utilizing a random forest model and yielded favorable classification results. Furthermore, we showed that decreased Anaerostipes_hadrus, Blautia_obeum, Blautia_wexlerae, Ruminococcus_torques and Dorea_longicatena could be associated with the reduced abundance of the gene encoding BSH (EC3.5.1.24). Additionally, increased Escherichia_coli contributed to the elevated abundance of the gene encoding 7-alpha-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase (EC1.1.1.159). These enzymes participate in bile acid metabolism, and bile acid levels may, therefore, be affected. Systemic immune activation in the pathogenesis of MDD, including increased of proinflammatory cytokines, is a well-recognized phenomenon [63]. In patients with MDD, GLCA and GUDCA were related to IL-17 and IL-6 levels, suggesting that the depletion of GUDCA and the enrichment of GLCA may be involved in the activation of the inflammatory response in depression. The bile acid profiles and gut microbiota reciprocally affect one another; microbiota modulate bile acid synthesis and the makeup of the bile acid pool, which subsequently act as potential regulators of gut microbiota composition and host metabolic processes [39, 64]. In summary, our results suggest that disturbances in the gut microbe-bile acid crosstalk may contribute to the pathophysiology of depression and are related to the inflammatory pathway.

BAs transformed by bacteria stimulate bile acid receptors, influencing signaling pathways. This extensive regulation spans complex networks of symbiotic metabolism, ultimately impacting brain function. To further explore the effect of abnormal BAs on cognitive function, Lasso penalized regression was used to identify BAs related to cognition function in patients with MDD after considering age, sex, BMI and education levels. The results showed that a disturbed bile acid spectrum affected multiple dimensions of cognitive function; GCDCA was negatively correlated with cognition. Recent research has reported that high levels of conjugated primary BAs and ammonia accumulation in the brain are correlated with cognitive impairment in age-related cognitive impairment [65]. GUDCA and TUDCA were strong positive contributors to cognition, whereas GLCA was a negative factor. In recent years, ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA), GUDCA, and TUDCA have been recognized for their anti-apoptotic, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and neuroprotective properties. A wealth of experimental and clinical data implied their potential utility as modifying agents in the treatment of neurodegenerative disorders [66–68]. In general, these compounds elicit their therapeutic effects through the suppression of neuroinflammation, the attenuation of oxidative stress, and the preservation of mitochondrial integrity. Elevated secondary BAs synthesized by bacteria, as well as their conjugated derivatives such as deoxycholic acid, glycodeoxycholic acid, taurodeoxycholic acid, and GLCA, have been observed in individuals with AD [22]. These cytotoxic secondary BAs, including DCA and its conjugates, can alter the integrity of the blood-brain barrier and accumulate in the brain. Disruptions in bile acid metabolism contribute to metabolic diseases, including liver diseases, cardiovascular diseases, and diabetes, all of which have direct or indirect links to an increased risk of cognitive deterioration [69–72]. These collective findings suggest that the disruption of microbiota-bile acid crosstalk may promote inflammation and MDD disease phenotypes, including changes in cognition. Based on the current associative evidence, we hypothesized that microbiota manipulation and supplementation with anti-inflammatory BAs may hold promise as a therapeutic avenue for cognitive impairment in MDD.

The following limitations should be noted. First, untargeted analysis of serum metabolomics, in which most of the metabolites could not be identified, hampered our research. We focused only on metabolites known to be relevant to gut microbiota and the brain, such as BAs and neurotransmitter-like metabolites, and overlooking certain serum metabolites that might be vital in the pathophysiology of MDD, warranting further investigation. Although we validated our results in another cohort using targeted metabolomics, the metagenomic results could not be verified. Ideally, the verification of microbes should be performed in future studies with larger sample sizes. The potential influence of low-dose intermittent medication use on our results deserves careful consideration; although reports suggest that such doses and frequencies are acceptable, the results should still be interpreted with caution. Second, despite methodological corrections, the cross-sectional design could only establish a correlation between gut microbiota, metabolites, and cognition in MDD; therefore, a causality inference cannot be inferred. Further, longitudinal studies are required to clarify the roles of the microbiome and BAs in the cognitive symptoms of MDD. Thirdly, limited by the operability of cognitive assessment tools, we did not assess all dimensions of cognitive function, such as hippocampus-dependent memory. More comprehensive tools should be selected in future studies. Finally, the specific mechanisms underlying the role of gut-bile acid-brain axis disturbance in cognitive impairment related to MDD should be further clarified using fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) in animal studies.

In summary, our study has made several important contributions, namely identifying abnormalities of gut microbes and bile acid metabolism in patients with MDD, elucidating the interaction between the gut microbiome, bile acid, and cognition, and highlighting changes in microbial enzyme activity in the bile acid metabolic pathway. These findings clarify that the gut-bile acid-brain axis network may be correlated with cognitive impairment in MDD, providing new perspectives on the fundamental pathophysiological processes. They identify potential targets for treating cognitive impairments in MDD and provide a basis for developing novel medications to improve cognitive symptoms in MDD patients.
